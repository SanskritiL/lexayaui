How it works:

When you invest money in stocks or index funds, you expect your money to grow based on interest over time. For US stocks, historical returns have been around 9% per year, with about 6% after inflation.

The Trinity Study looked at a wide range of market performance over past years, and found that if one withdrew at a 4% rate of their total remaining savings, they would have a very low change of running out of money.

This leads to the helpful rule of thumb:

Savings Required = Yearly Spending / Withdrawal Rate. A lower withdrawal rate means you'll need to save more, but it also reduces the risk of running out of money in retirement.

Assumptions:

  • Your savings are invested in low cost index funds which track the US stock markets (ex: VTI).
  • The 4% withdrawal rate is calculated based on 15 to 30 year retirement periods. For longer retirement periods, use a lower withdrawal rate to decrease your changes of running out of money.
  • The future performance of US stocks will be similar to the past. Not true when looking at individual stocks, but surprisingly consistent for an index longer term.
